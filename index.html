I am a PhD candidate in the Bioinformatics and Computational Biology Program at the University of Idaho. My dissertation takes a multidisciplinary approach to study infectious disease. Specifically, I have developed mathematical models to study the impact of transmissible vaccines to control infectious diseases in wildlife populations, and have compiled databases and developed web interfaces to visualize environmental and infectious disease data.

Recent Projects

Environmental Database and Mapping

The environmental conditions of local habitats determine the extent to which pathogens and their reservoir species are able to persist. I have developed a database that compiles environmental raster data over space and time across West Africa. The database provides modelers and other researchers with up-to-date environmental variables across the study region that are well-formatted for model integration.

Modeling Transmissible Vaccines

Transmissible vaccines—vaccines capable of spreading from animal to animal—are a novel tool for limiting the spread of zoonotic pathogens within wildlife reservoirs. While theoretical models of transmissible vaccines highlight their potential, such have not yet been formulated for specific vaccine candidates. I have paired epidemiological models of transmissible vaccines with seroprevalence data to provide the first estimates for the effectiveness of transmissible vaccines for a specific vaccine design.

Lassa Virus Database and Dashboard

We have compiled published studies documenting the presence of Lassa virus across West Africa and integrated these data into an interactive GIS database that can be visualized with our Lassa virus dashboard.
